🎯 Talk Theme Overview

This 10-minute talk draws on Jeremiah chapters 4–6 to examine three symptoms of Judah’s spiritual sickness — and asks the probing question: “How can I avoid becoming spiritually sick?” Like a doctor who gives an accurate diagnosis, Jehovah identified Israel’s problem precisely. If we were seriously ill physically, we’d want an accurate diagnosis. Spiritually, we should want the same.

💭 ASK YOURSELF: “If I were seriously ill, I would want an accurate diagnosis so I could be treated in time. Spiritually speaking, would I not want the same?”

Point 1 — Stubborn & Rebellious Heart (Jer 4:4, 14)

Jeremiah 4:4
“Circumcise yourselves to Jehovah, and remove the foreskins of your hearts, you men of Judah and inhabitants of Jerusalem, so that my wrath may not blaze up like a fire and burn with no one to extinguish it, because of your evil deeds.”
Jeremiah 4:14
“Wash your heart clean of wickedness, O Jerusalem, in order to be saved. How long will you harbor wicked thoughts?”

From w13 3/15 ¶8-9: To ‘remove the foreskin of the heart’ meant getting rid of whatever made their heart unresponsive to God — thinking, affections, or motives in conflict with His. Their heart was stubbornly rebellious. We too must examine our figurative heart for attitudes that may be making us less sensitive to Jehovah’s guidance.

🔍 Spiritual Gems Question

Jer 4:10 — In what sense did Jehovah deceive his people? (w07 3/15 p.9 ¶4)
Jehovah did not literally deceive His people. Jeremiah’s words here are an expression of anguish and astonishment — a lament. False prophets had long told the people “You will have peace” (see Jer 6:14), a message Jehovah had permitted to continue as part of His judicial dealings with a rebellious nation. From Jeremiah’s distressed perspective, it felt like deception because the people had trusted these false assurances. But Jehovah had consistently sent true watchmen like Jeremiah with warnings. The people’s own willingness to believe comfortable lies — rather than Jehovah’s true prophets — was the real cause of the tragedy.

📋 Passage Summary

Jeremiah roams Jerusalem searching for even one faithful person — searching both common people (vv.1-5) and prominent men (v.5) — finding none. All have hardened their hearts and broken the covenant. After repeated corrections, they still refused to repent. Jehovah asks rhetorically: “Should I not call them to account?” (v.9). The passage concludes with the charge of treachery against both Israel and Judah (v.11).

🎯 th Study 5 — Logical Organization

What this quality means: Let the logical flow of the passage be clear in how you read. Group related ideas, maintain deliberate pacing through Jeremiah’s fruitless search (vv.1-5), build toward the drama of Jehovah’s rhetorical question in verse 9, and express the sadness of the treachery charge in verse 11. Structure: Search → Failure → God’s response → Charge of treachery.
Jeremiah 5:3 — Key verse to emphasize
“O Jehovah, do your eyes not look for faithfulness? You struck them, but it made no impact on them. You exterminated them, but they refused to accept discipline. They made their faces harder than a rock, and they refused to turn around.”

📚 lmd Lesson 2 — Naturalness (Point 3)

Principle: “A word spoken at the right time — how good it is!” (Prov. 15:23)

📚 lmd Lesson 1 — Interest in Others (Point 3)

Principle: “Love does not look for its own interests.” (1 Cor. 13:4, 5)

Point 3 — Be Flexible: Do not insist on starting with the topic you had in mind. Start with something the person is already thinking about. Ask yourself: ‘What is in the news? What are my neighbors talking about? What might this person be thinking about right now?’ Also: listen — don’t talk too much; encourage the other person to express himself.

Full Sample Script — House to House (lmd Lesson 1, Point 3)
Publisher: “Good morning! My name is [Name]. I’m one of Jehovah’s Witnesses visiting the neighborhood today. I won’t take much of your time.” ← lmd 1 pt.3: Be observant before knocking. Note any indicators by the door or yard that could open a natural conversation. A warm, non-threatening greeting sets the right tone.
Householder: “Oh — okay.”
Publisher: “With everything going on in the world lately, do you feel like things are getting better or worse?” ← lmd 1 pt.3: Be flexible. An open-ended question about something current shows genuine interest in their perspective, not just your message.
Householder: “Honestly? Worse. I worry about my kids’ future.”
Publisher: “I completely understand that. Many parents feel exactly the same way. That’s actually something the Bible addresses — not just why things are difficult, but a real hope for the future. They told you their concern — connect your message directly to it. Showing you actually heard them is what creates genuine interest.

📋 Talk Key Points (ijwfq article 5)

Isaiah 43:10-12
“‘You are my witnesses,’ declares Jehovah, ‘yes, my servant whom I have chosen, so that you may know and have faith in me and understand that I am the same One. Before me no God was formed, and after me there has been none. I — I am Jehovah, and besides me there is no savior.’”

Three main points to cover:

1. The Name “Jehovah” — Jehovah is God’s personal name, appearing some 7,000 times in the Bible. (Exodus 6:3; Psalm 83:18) Using His name is not presumptuous — it’s what He invites His worshippers to do.

2. What a “Witness” Is — A witness is someone who testifies to truths they know firsthand. Jehovah told ancient Israel: “You are my witnesses” (Isa 43:10). The name describes what we do, not just who we are.

3. What the Name Together Means — “Jehovah’s Witnesses” = people who proclaim truth about Jehovah, the Creator of all things (Rev 4:11). We witness by how we live and by sharing what we’ve learned from the Bible. (1 Pet 2:12)

th Study 7 — Illustrating Artfully: Use a simple, concrete illustration. For example: just as a courtroom witness testifies to what they’ve personally seen, a “witness” for Jehovah testifies to what they know about Him from the Bible. This makes the name immediately understandable to any householder.

Publisher: “Have you ever been asked: ‘Why do you call yourselves Jehovah’s Witnesses?’ It’s a great question. The answer becomes very clear once you break down both parts of the name...” ← th study 7: Open with a relatable hook the audience recognizes. Then use the two-part illustration to logically unfold the name’s meaning in a way the householder can easily follow.

🎬 VIDEO: “Protect Yourself From Misinformation”

📹 Video Synopsis

This JW.org video addresses the growing problem of misinformation in our digital age, including manipulated images, misleading headlines, and the phenomenon of “deepfakes” — AI-generated audio and video content that appears authentic but is completely fabricated. With deepfake technology advancing rapidly, it is now possible to create realistic-seeming video of a real person saying something they never said.

The video draws a parallel to ancient Israel: false prophets told people what they wanted to hear (Jer 5:31; Isa 30:10) rather than what was true. People today can similarly be misled by content designed to provoke outrage, fear, or excitement — content that appeals to emotions and confirms pre-existing biases.

The key principle is from Job 12:11: “Does not the ear test out words as the palate tastes food?” Just as we taste food before swallowing it, we should test information before accepting or sharing it. Practical steps from the video: pause before reacting emotionally, verify the original source, check multiple credible outlets, and ask “Why is this being shared?” before forwarding.

❓ Discussion Questions — Prepared Answers

1. Why is misinformation dangerous?
Misinformation can distort our view of reality, damage relationships, and erode our trust in reliable spiritual sources. When we base decisions on false information, the consequences can be spiritually, emotionally, and practically harmful. In ancient Judah, false prophets spread a feel-good message of “peace” when there was no peace (Jer 5:31). The result was catastrophic. Similarly today, misinformation can make us doubt genuine spiritual guidance from Jehovah’s organization, develop unfounded fears, or hold grudges based on false reports. It’s dangerous not just intellectually but spiritually.
💬 Comment on Q1

“Misinformation doesn’t just confuse us intellectually — it can actually separate us from Jehovah if we let it shape how we view His organization or His Word. That makes it a spiritual issue, not just an information management issue.”

2. What is deepfake technology, and why do we need to be aware of it?
Deepfakes are artificially generated or manipulated audio and video created using AI tools. They can make it appear that a real person said or did something they never actually said or did — with increasing realism that makes them very difficult to detect with the naked eye. We need to be aware because in an age when we instinctively trust what we see and hear, this technology can be weaponized to spread lies with unusual convincing power. For Christians, this reinforces the need to rely on trusted, verifiable sources — the Bible, jw.org, and congregation elders — rather than internet content alone.
💬 Comment on Q2

“Learning about deepfakes actually made me more grateful for Jehovah’s Word — it doesn’t change, it can’t be manipulated, and we can always verify it. In a world where even video can’t always be trusted, that stability is genuinely priceless.”

3. How can we put information to the test? — Job 12:11
Job 12:11
“Does not the ear test out words as the palate tastes food?”
Just as we taste food before swallowing it, we should test information before accepting or sharing it. Practical steps: (1) Pause before reacting — strong emotions (outrage, shock, excitement) signal that our bias may be engaged. (2) Verify the original source — is it reliable and independently verifiable? (3) Check multiple credible sources. (4) Ask “Why is this being shared?” — is it designed to inform or to provoke? (5) For spiritual matters, consult jw.org or congregation elders rather than relying on internet rumors.
💬 Comment on Q3

“The illustration from Job 12:11 is perfect — we wouldn’t eat food without checking whether it’s safe first. Applying that same practical caution to what we read and share online is just wisdom, and I love that Jehovah inspired that principle long before the internet existed.”

💭 ASK YOURSELF: “Do I tend to trust only information that confirms what I want to believe? Or do I keep my biases in check?”

CBS Lesson 92
Jesus Appears to the Fishermen
John 21:1-19, 25 Acts 1:1-3 Matt 4:19, 20
▾
Jesus prepares breakfast for his disciples on the shore of the Sea of Galilee
🖼️ Image Application

Notice that Jesus had the fire going and breakfast prepared before the disciples arrived — He had already provided for their practical need. Even as the resurrected Son of God, Jesus showed humble, thoughtful care for others. This reflects Jehovah’s character: He anticipates our needs and provides, often before we even ask. It also illustrates how true greatness in God’s eyes involves serving, not being served.

Peter jumps into the water to swim to Jesus; the other disciples follow in the boat
🖼️ Image Application

Peter’s impulsive jump into the water shows his deep eagerness to be with Jesus — he couldn’t wait for the boat. Though Peter had denied Jesus three times just weeks earlier, his love remained strong. This teaches us that past failures do not disqualify us from a wholehearted relationship with Jehovah and Jesus. What matters is that we return and keep following.

📖 Lesson Narrative

After the resurrection, Peter and several disciples went fishing on the Sea of Galilee. They fished all night and caught nothing. At dawn, a man on the shore asked if they had caught anything — they had not. He told them to cast the net on the right side of the boat. The net immediately filled with so many fish they could not haul it in. John realized: “It is the Lord!” Peter jumped into the water and swam to shore. On the beach, Jesus had bread and fish cooking on a fire. He invited them to breakfast.

After eating, Jesus had an intimate exchange with Peter. Three times he asked: “Do you love me?” Three times Peter affirmed his love. Each time, Jesus commissioned him: “Feed my lambs / Shepherd my little sheep / Feed my little sheep.” Jesus also foretold how Peter would die, and said: “Continue following me.”

Matthew 4:19-20 — Key Verse
“[Jesus] said to them: ‘Come after me, and I will make you fishers of men.’ At once they abandoned their nets and followed him.”

❓ Printed Questions

What miracle did Jesus do for the fishermen? ▾
Jesus directed the disciples to cast their net on the right side of the boat after a fruitless night of fishing, resulting in such a massive catch that they could not even pull the net aboard. This miracle was reminiscent of an earlier miraculous catch at the beginning of Jesus’ ministry (Luke 5:1-11), which would have been deeply meaningful to Peter, James, and John — reminding them of how they first began following Jesus.
Why, do you think, did Jesus three times ask Peter: “Do you love me?” ▾
Peter had denied Jesus three times (John 18:15-27). By giving Peter three opportunities to affirm his love, Jesus gently helped him process the guilt of those denials and restored him publicly. Each affirmation was paired with a commission — “Feed my lambs / Shepherd my little sheep / Feed my little sheep” — showing that love for Jesus must be expressed through loving service to others. Jesus also foretold Peter’s martyrdom, further demonstrating confidence in Peter’s faithfulness going forward.

💡 Wrap-Up Questions

What does this lesson teach us about Jehovah? ▾
This lesson shows that Jehovah’s forgiveness and restoration are complete and tender. Through Jesus, Jehovah reached out to a disciple who had failed — not to condemn him, but to recommission him. Jehovah does not define us by our worst moments. He sees our love and our potential, and He patiently restores those who sincerely want to serve Him. (Ps 103:13, 14) Additionally, “Feed my sheep” shows that Jehovah values love expressed through caring for others — not merely through words or feelings.
Are there any points from this lesson we can use in the ministry or daily life? ▾
In the ministry: Jesus used a practical, everyday situation — fishing — to teach and reach his disciples. Similarly, we can start conversations from everyday contexts and concerns. He also met them where they were, physically and emotionally, rather than calling them somewhere formal.

In daily life: When we have failed or feel spiritually distant, Peter’s restoration encourages us not to give up. Jesus specifically sought out Peter after the resurrection. If we sincerely love Jehovah, He will find ways to restore and recommission us. Also, love for Jesus is shown concretely through how we treat and care for those around us — “the flock.”

CBS Lesson 93
Jesus Returns to Heaven
Matt 28:16-20 Acts 1:2-14 1 Cor 15:3-6 Matt 24:14
▾
Jesus ascends to heaven as his disciples watch from the Mount of Olives
🖼️ Image Application

The disciples’ upward gaze after Jesus disappears into the cloud captures a pivotal transition — from Jesus’ visible presence to guidance through his spirit. In our lives we face similar transitions: when visible support seems to disappear, when prayers seem unanswered, when we must trust without seeing. This image reminds us that Jesus’ bodily absence was not abandonment. He promised: “I am with you all the days.” (Matt 28:20) The disciples returned to Jerusalem with joy (Luke 24:52) because they trusted that promise — and we can too.

📖 Lesson Narrative

During the 40 days following his resurrection, Jesus appeared to hundreds of followers — over 500 at one time (1 Cor 15:6) — teaching and performing miracles. In Galilee he gave the Great Commission: “Go and make disciples of people from all lands. Teach them the things I taught you, and baptize them.” He promised: “I will always be with you.” (Matt 28:19-20)

At the final meeting on the Mount of Olives, when the apostles asked if Jesus was now restoring the kingdom to Israel, he redirected them: “It is not yet Jehovah’s time. But you will receive power from holy spirit and be my witnesses — in Jerusalem, Judea, Samaria, and to the farthest parts of the earth.” He was then lifted up and a cloud covered him. Two angels reassured the watching disciples that Jesus would return in the same manner. The disciples went back to Jerusalem, meeting daily in the upper room with prayer, waiting for the promised holy spirit.

Matthew 24:14 — Key Verse
“This good news of the Kingdom will be preached in all the inhabited earth for a witness to all the nations, and then the end will come.”

❓ Printed Questions

What command did Jesus give to his disciples? ▾
Jesus gave two interconnected commands: (1) The Great Commission in Galilee — go, make disciples of all nations, baptize, and teach (Matt 28:19-20); and (2) On the Mount of Olives — wait in Jerusalem for the holy spirit, then be witnesses “to the farthest parts of the earth” (Acts 1:4-8). Together, these commands define the primary mission: global, spirit-empowered disciple-making — the same work carried out by Jehovah’s Witnesses today.
What happened on the Mount of Olives? ▾
Jesus met his apostles for the final time on the Mount of Olives. He instructed them to wait in Jerusalem for holy spirit, redirected their question about the kingdom’s timing, and commissioned them as global witnesses. Then he was “lifted up” and a cloud took him out of their sight. Two angels appeared and promised he would return in the same manner. The disciples then returned to Jerusalem with joy (Luke 24:52), meeting daily in the upper room in prayer with about 120 disciples.

💡 Wrap-Up Questions

What does this lesson teach us about Jehovah? ▾
This lesson shows that Jehovah follows through on His promises — He had promised to send holy spirit (John 14:16-17), and He did at Pentecost. It also shows Jehovah’s confidence in imperfect humans: despite the apostles’ misunderstanding about the kingdom’s timing, Jesus patiently redirected them and trusted them with the most important mission in history. Jehovah works through ordinary people empowered by His spirit to accomplish extraordinary things. Matt 24:14 — still being fulfilled today — shows His desire that all people have the opportunity to know Him.
Are there any points from this lesson we can use in the ministry or daily life? ▾
In the ministry: The Great Commission applies directly to us. Matthew 24:14 is being fulfilled in our generation. Each conversation in the ministry — each Bible study, each returned call — is a small piece of that global fulfillment. That makes even one meaningful conversation significant in a cosmic sense.

In daily life: The disciples waited together in prayer — unity and patience during a time of transition. When we face waiting seasons — answers to prayer, changed circumstances — we can follow their example: gather with fellow believers, pray, and trust Jehovah’s timing. Also, “I will always be with you” (Matt 28:20) is a personal promise we can claim for ourselves every day.
