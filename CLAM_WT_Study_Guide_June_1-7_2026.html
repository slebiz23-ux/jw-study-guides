2. Spiritual Gems
Discussion · 10 min · Jer 2:28

Q: What can we learn from Jehovah's challenge to the unfaithful Judeans? (Jer 2:28; jr p.43 ¶3) ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
Jehovah sarcastically challenged Israel to let their many idols "rise up and save" them in their time of crisis. They had "as many gods as cities" — yet none of those gods could actually help them. This powerfully demonstrates the futility of false worship. Only Jehovah is the true God who provides real, reliable help. For us today, it also warns against anything that functions as a modern "idol" — something we trust in or depend on more than Jehovah, such as financial security, human relationships, or our own abilities.
💬 Comment you could share: "I was struck by Jehovah's challenge at Jeremiah 2:28. He essentially said — 'Go ahead and ask your idols for help, and see what happens.' Only Jehovah can, and that makes me grateful to be on his side."
📌 Ministry connection: "This scripture could be a powerful conversation opener with someone who says their religion doesn't matter much as long as they live a good life. Jehovah himself says it matters very much who we worship — there is a real difference between the one True God who can save and idols that cannot."

8. "Make a Defense . . . With a Mild Temper and Deep Respect"
Discussion · 9 min · 1 Pet 3:15
▾

🎯 Theme

We must be bold in declaring the good news and defending Jehovah's moral standards — but we balance boldness with mild temper and deep respect for the person.

Q: What other qualities are important when explaining our beliefs? (1 Pet 3:15) ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
Along with boldness, Peter says: mild temper (gentleness, not anger or defensiveness) and deep respect (treating the person with dignity even when they disagree). Additional qualities: humility (not having all the answers), empathy (genuinely trying to understand their perspective), patience (not expecting a change of view immediately), and love (caring about the person above the outcome of the conversation).
💬 Comment you could share: "Reading 1 Peter 3:15, I noticed that Peter didn't just say be bold — he said be bold with mild temper and deep respect. To me that means those qualities need to be present at the same time, not in sequence. It's not: 'be bold first, then be respectful if they push back.' It's all together. I want to work on keeping that balance — being firm on the truth without becoming tense or confrontational."
Q: Why are these qualities important? ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
Because our goal is not to win arguments but to reach hearts — and hearts are reached through love, not force. If we respond with anger or condescension, we push people away from Jehovah. Our conduct also directly reflects on Jehovah himself — a harsh response gives people a distorted picture of what serving him looks like. The right qualities can even move opposers to eventually respect and admire us.
💬 Comment you could share: "I think these qualities matter because we're representing Jehovah in every conversation. When I've stayed calm and respectful even when someone was being dismissive, I've sometimes noticed their tone shift mid-conversation. It's like the mild temper we show disarms the tension. And even if they don't change their mind right then, at least they walk away with a positive impression of Jehovah's Witnesses."

Q: What did you learn from the video to help explain your beliefs about homosexuality tactfully? ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
Key principles: (1) Separate the person from the position — respect and love the individual regardless of lifestyle. (2) Explain the source of our standard — it comes from love for Jehovah and trust in his wisdom, not personal judgment of others. (3) Own the position without apology or aggression. (4) Focus on what the Bible teaches rather than your personal opinion — this shifts the conversation from "me vs. you" to "what God's Word actually says."

What I took from it is the importance of letting the person know I genuinely respect them as a person — that my view comes from Scripture and love for Jehovah, not from any negative feeling toward them. That keeps it non-threatening and focused on Jehovah's standard, not my opinion."

Why we can be happy when we are hated for serving Jehovah.

Why We Are Hated — ¶1–10
Introduction + first main section
▾
¶ 1
Q1: What surprising statement did Jesus make about being hated? ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
In his Sermon on the Mount, Jesus said: "Happy are you whenever men hate you." (Luke 6:22) This was surprising because no one naturally enjoys being disliked or excluded. Yet Jesus made this statement because his followers would face hatred — and there would be genuine reasons to be happy even in the midst of it.
💬 Comment you could share: "I found this verse striking right from the opening. Jesus didn't say 'tolerate' being hated or 'endure' it — he said be happy. That's a completely different attitude. It made me want to understand why he would say that, and the article gives very real, satisfying reasons. What Jesus said isn't blind optimism — it's based on solid truth."
¶ 2–3
Q2-3 (a): What is one reason why true Christians are persecuted? (b): How might this affect our view of opposers? (John 16:2, 3) ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answers
(a) We are hated because we worship Jehovah. Satan — "the god of this system of things" (2 Cor. 4:3, 4) — blinds people to the truth about Jehovah and moves them to oppose those who love and serve him. (John 8:42-44)

(b) Knowing Satan is ultimately behind the opposition helps us avoid hating our opposers in return. Like the imprisoned brother Pavel, who recognized most prison staff had no personal animosity — they were doing what they believed they should do, under Satan's broader influence. Seeing opposers as deceived rather than malicious helps us respond with compassion rather than bitterness.
💬 Comment you could share: "The point about Satan being the real enemy — not the person opposing us — genuinely changed how I think about opposition. When a family member or workmate says something unkind about my faith, it's easy to feel personally hurt. But remembering that Satan is behind it shifts my emotion from resentment toward compassion for the person. They're being misled. That actually makes it easier for me to still care about them."
📌 Ministry connection: "This is also something I keep in mind when Bible students face opposition from family members. When I can help them see that their family member isn't the real enemy, it reduces the personal hurt and helps them respond with love instead of defensiveness."
¶ 4
Q4: What can we learn from the examples of Jesus and of the disciple Stephen? (See also picture.) ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
Both Jesus and Stephen prayed for forgiveness for those who were executing them. Jesus prayed "Father, forgive them" while being nailed to the torture stake (Luke 23:34); Stephen asked God to forgive his executioners (Acts 7:58-60). The lesson: we do not hate our opposers — we may even be moved to pray for them. And Jehovah answered those prayers. Many involved in Jesus' execution later repented and got baptized (Acts 2:36-41), and Saul — who approved Stephen's death — became the apostle Paul.
🖼️ Picture (¶4): Shows Jesus and Stephen each praying for those persecuting them. Application: This image reinforces that choosing prayer over bitterness is not weakness — it is the highest form of spiritual courage, practiced by both our Lord and an early disciple. When I look at this picture, I'm reminded that no opposer is beyond Jehovah's reach, and that praying for them is one of the most powerful things I can do.
💬 Comment you could share: "The examples of Jesus and Stephen in paragraph 4 are challenging in the most positive way. Both of them — under the worst possible circumstances — thought of forgiveness, not retaliation. What strikes me is that their prayers were answered: some of those very people later became disciples. It gives me real hope that praying for those who oppose me isn't wishful thinking — Jehovah can use those prayers to reach people I can't reach myself."
¶ 5
Q5: What do you learn from César's experience? ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
César's experience shows the power of consistent, respectful conduct combined with persistent prayer for an opposer. His mother prioritized the Kingdom while never neglecting her husband, and taught her children to respect him even in his opposition. Over years, the family's loving behavior gradually softened a violent father. After a heartfelt prayer, César offered to study the Bible with his father — who said yes, and was eventually baptized. Lesson: not all opposers change quickly, but sustained Christlike conduct can make a real difference over time.
💬 Comment you could share: "César's story in paragraph 5 encouraged me because it shows that consistent conduct over a long period can reach even a hardened opposer. His mother never gave up showing love while maintaining her integrity. What stood out to me was that it was a heartfelt prayer that opened the door to the Bible study conversation. It reminds me that prayer is not just preparation — it can be the turning point itself."
📌 Ministry application: "This experience is one I might share with a Bible student whose family member is strongly opposed. It shows that their patient, loving example — not arguments — is often what makes the lasting difference."
¶ 6
Q6: According to Mark 13:13, what is another reason why we are hated? ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
We are hated "on account of [Jesus'] name" (Mark 13:13) — his reputation and his authority as King of God's Kingdom. People who trust in earthly leaders resent our proclamation that Jesus has been ruling as King since 1914 and will soon replace all rival governments. Our message directly challenges where people place their ultimate loyalty.
💬 Comment you could share: "Paragraph 6 helped me see that being hated 'on account of Jesus' name' is actually tied to the Kingdom hope — our message that Jesus is already ruling. People who are heavily invested in this world's systems find that message threatening, because it implies those systems are temporary. I see this as a privilege, not just a burden — it means our preaching is actually landing with impact."
¶ 7–8
Q7-8: Why do some of Jehovah's people become targets of ridicule? (John 15:18-20) (See also pictures.) ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
We are targets of ridicule because we "are no part of the world" (John 15:18-20) — we reject ungodly attitudes, conduct, and speech. (1 Pet. 4:3, 4) Like Ignacio, who faced years of mockery for his high moral standards, our consistent stand can make us conspicuous. Yet his story shows the ultimate outcome — the teacher who mocked him for years attended meetings and was eventually baptized, moved by the consistency of Ignacio's conduct.
🖼️ Picture (¶8): Shows Ignacio witnessing to his teacher with the caption "Regardless of our age, we can courageously and successfully defend our faith." Application: This picture speaks especially to those who face pressure at school or work. It shows that age doesn't limit our ability to make an impact — and that the person in front of us (even an authority figure) may be more open than they appear. The "witness" we give through our daily conduct is as powerful as the words we say.
💬 Comment you could share: "The experience of Ignacio in paragraphs 7 and 8 is one I find really encouraging. He endured years of mockery and never compromised. What moved me is that the teacher — the very one who ridiculed him — is the one who approached Ignacio at the end and asked how he stayed consistent. Our faithfulness makes people curious. Even when they're mocking on the outside, they may be observing and wondering on the inside."
¶ 9–10
Q9-10 (a): For what other reason do Christians stand out as different from Satan's world? (b): What can we learn from the apostle Paul's example? ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answers
(a) We are politically neutral — we do not run for office or vote, because our allegiance is to Jehovah and his Kingdom under Christ. (John 18:36; Rom. 13:1) This makes us stand out in a heavily politicized world and sometimes brings opposition and imprisonment.

(b) Paul preached to everyone while under arrest — guards, governors, court officials. He viewed imprisonment not as punishment but as an assignment from Jehovah to find sheeplike individuals in places he would never otherwise reach. (Col. 4:3)
💬 Comment you could share: "What Paul's example in paragraph 10 shows me is that no circumstance — not even imprisonment — stops the good news from spreading if we have the right heart. He didn't see his chains as a limitation but as a platform. I want to have that attitude about whatever circumstances I face — instead of thinking 'this isn't the right time or place to witness,' looking for the opportunity within the situation."

Why We Can Be Happy When Hated — ¶11–17
Main application section — three key reasons
▾
¶ 11
Q11: How can experiencing persecution strengthen our faith? Give an example. ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
Persecution confirms that Bible prophecy is being fulfilled, which is a source of happiness rather than shock. Jehovah foretold from Genesis 3:15 onward that those following him would be hated. (Gen. 3:15; Matt. 10:22; 2 Tim. 3:12) When opposition arises, it assures us we are serving the true God. The sister whose husband burned her Bible grew stronger in faith — persecution "convinced me that this was the true religion."
💬 Comment you could share: "Paragraph 11 gave me a new way of thinking about opposition. Instead of asking 'Why is this happening to me?', I can ask 'Why wouldn't this happen to me — didn't Jehovah say it would?' When I see my trials in the light of fulfilled prophecy, they stop feeling random and start feeling like confirmation that I'm on the right path. That actually produces real happiness, not just resignation."
¶ 12
Q12: What helped one brother to cope with persecution? ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
Two interconnected practices gave him peace: (1) Constant, structured prayer — starting each morning in prayer, praying when difficulties arose, and praying specifically when injustices made him angry. (2) Meditation on examples of integrity — both biblical figures and modern-day brothers and sisters. Together, these produced the peace Jesus promised. (John 14:27; 16:33)
💬 Comment you could share: "The brother in paragraph 12 described locking himself in the bathroom to pray when he was angry — and I found that so relatable and so practical. He didn't suppress his emotions; he brought them honestly to Jehovah. That's something I've been trying to do more — instead of waiting until I feel 'spiritual enough' to pray, coming to Jehovah exactly as I am, including when I'm frustrated or hurt. That kind of honest prayer has helped me maintain peace in ways I didn't expect."
🙏 Getting to know Jehovah: "What this paragraph shows me about Jehovah is that he wants us to bring our full, honest selves to him in prayer — anger, sadness, confusion included. He doesn't require us to be composed before approaching him. That's the kind of Father he is, and learning that has deepened my personal relationship with him."
¶ 13
Q13: What can help us to conquer hatred? ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
Love — deep love for both Jehovah and for our brothers and sisters — can help us conquer hatred. Jesus loved his Father and his friends completely, right to his last breath. (John 13:1; 15:13) When love fills our hearts, there is less room for bitterness to take hold. Love is not passive — it is an active force that displaces hatred.
💬 Comment you could share: "The idea in paragraph 13 that love is 'stronger than hatred' is not just poetic — it's practical. I've noticed that when I'm regularly building my relationship with Jehovah and spending time with my spiritual family, opposition from the outside genuinely bothers me less. The love on the inside is louder than the hatred on the outside. I think that's what Jesus was modeling when he said to love our enemies — it's not about pretending the opposition doesn't hurt, but about having something stronger filling our hearts."
¶ 14
Q14: What helped Paul to stand firm in the face of death? ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
Paul wrote: "God did not give us a spirit of cowardice, but one of power and of love." (2 Tim. 1:7) His deep personal love for Jehovah empowered him to face death without abandoning true worship. This was not bravado — it was faith-powered courage rooted in his intimate relationship with Jehovah. (Acts 20:22-24)
💬 Comment you could share: "What I took from paragraph 14 is that Paul's courage came from his relationship with Jehovah, not from a natural fearless personality. He had fear — that's why he needed to remind himself that Jehovah gave him 'a spirit of power and of love, not of cowardice.' The antidote to cowardice, according to Paul, was love — love for Jehovah specifically. That gives me something to work on: the more I deepen my relationship with Jehovah, the more courage I'll have when I need it."

💬 Comment you could share: "The picture and the experience in paragraph 15 moved me. It made me think about who in my congregation might be going through something difficult right now, and whether I'm making my support felt in a tangible way, not just in thoughts or prayers."
¶ 16
Q16: Why did the apostle Peter say that those mistreated for serving God have reason to be happy? (1 Peter 4:14) ▸ Show answer

Peter said that mistreatment for serving God is proof that "the spirit of glory — yes, the spirit of God — is resting upon you" — meaning Jehovah's approval rests on those who endure. Peter lived this himself — after being arrested and flogged, he and the apostles rejoiced because they had been "counted worthy to be dishonored in behalf of Jesus' name," and immediately kept preaching. 💬 Comment you could share: "First Peter 4:14 gives a perspective I want to hold onto: mistreatment for Jehovah's name isn't just something to endure — it's actually evidence that Jehovah's spirit is actively resting on me. Peter and the apostles walked out of a flogging and rejoiced. I'm not being flogged, but whatever opposition I face, I can apply the same logic: this is proof Jehovah sees me and his spirit is with me. That makes facing opposition feel completely different."
🙏 Getting to know Jehovah: "What I love about this point is what it tells us about Jehovah — he doesn't overlook what we go through in his name. ¶ 17
Q17: What did Jesus tell his disciples on the night before his death? ▸ Show answer
📝 Suggested Answer
